Gases and solutions

We will begin by taking a closer look at gases and solutions and work out how to solve problems relating to them.

Molar volumes of gases

It is possible to calculate the volume of one mole of gas at standard temperature and pressure (STP) using what we now know about gases.

STP is a temperature of CLICKME\text{273}~CLICKME\text{K} and a pressure of CLICKME\text{101,3}~CLICKME\text{kPa}. The amount of gas is usually CLICKME\text{1}~CLICKME\text{mol}.

We write down all the values that we know about one mole of gas at STP:

CLICKME\begin{align*} p & = \text{101,3}\text{ kPa} = \text{101\ 300}\text{ Pa} \\ n & = \text{1}\text{ mol} \\ R & = \text{8,31}\text{ J·K^{-1}·mol^{-1}} \\ T & = \text{273}\text{ K} \end{align*}

Now we can substitute these values into the ideal gas equation:

CLICKME\begin{align*} pV & = nRT \\ (\text{101\ 300})V & = (\text{1})(\text{8,31})(\text{273}) \\ (\text{101\ 300})V & = \text{2265,9} \\ V & = \text{0,0224}\text{ m^{3}} \\ V & = \text{22,4}\text{ dm^{3}} \end{align*}

The volume of 1 mole of gas at STP is CLICKME\text{22,4}~CLICKME\text{dm^{3}}.

And if we had any number of moles of gas, not just one mole then we would get: CLICKME\boxed{V_{g} = \text{22,4}n_{g}}

The standard units used for this equation are p in CLICKME\text{Pa}, V in CLICKME\text{m^{3}} and T in CLICKME\text{K}. Remember also that CLICKME\text{1000}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}}CLICKME=CLICKME\text{1}~CLICKME\text{dm^{3}} and CLICKME\text{1000}~CLICKME\text{dm^{3}}CLICKME=CLICKME\text{1}~CLICKME\text{m^{3}}.

Reactions and gases

Some reactions take place between gases. For these reactions we can work out the volumes of the gases using the fact that volume is proportional to the number of moles.

We can use the following formula:

CLICKME\begin{align*} V_{\textrm{A}} & = \frac{a}{b} V_{\textrm{B}} \end{align*}

where:

CLICKME\begin{align*} V_{A} & = \textrm{volume of A}\\ V_{B} & = \textrm{volume of B}\\ a & = \textrm{stoichiometric coefficient of A}\\ b & = \textrm{stoichiometric coefficient of B} \end{align*}

The number in front of a reactant or a product in a balanced chemical equation is called the stoichiometric coefficient or stoichiometric ratio.

Volume and gases

Hydrogen and oxygen react to form water according to the following equation:

CLICKME2\textrm{H}_{2} \textrm{(g)} + \textrm{O}_{2} \textrm{(g)} \rightarrow 2\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O} \textrm{(g)}

If CLICKME\text{3}~CLICKME\text{dm^{3}} of oxygen is used, what volume of water is produced?

Determine the volume of water produced in the reaction.

We use the equation given above to work out the volume of water needed: CLICKME\begin{align*} V_{A} & = \frac{a}{b}V_{B} \\ V_{\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O}} & = \frac{2}{1}V_{\textrm{O}_{2}} \\ & = 2(\text{3})\\ & = \text{6}\text{ dm^{3}} \end{align*}

We can interpret the chemical equation in the worked example above (CLICKME2\textrm{H}_{2} \textrm{(g)} + \textrm{O}_{2} \textrm{(g)} \rightarrow 2\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O} \textrm{(g)}) as:

CLICKME\text{2} moles of hydrogen react with CLICKME\text{1} mole of oxygen to produce CLICKME\text{2} moles of water. We can also say that CLICKME\text{2} volumes of hydrogen react with CLICKME\text{1} volume of oxygen to produce CLICKME\text{2} volumes of water.

Solutions

In grade 10 you learnt how to calculate the molar concentration of a solution. The molar concentration of a solution is the number of moles of solute per litre of solvent (CLICKME\text{mol·L^{-1}}). This is more commonly given as moles of solute per cubic decimetre of solution (CLICKME\text{mol·dm^{-3}}).

To calculate concentration we use CLICKMEC = \frac{n}{V}, where CLICKMEC is the molar concentration, CLICKMEn is the number of moles and CLICKMEV is the volume of the solution.

Calculating molar concentrations is useful to determine how much solute we need to add to a given volume of solvent in order to make a standard solution.

A standard solution is a solution in which the concentration is known to a high degree of precision. When we work with standard solutions we can take the concentration to be constant.

When you are busy with these calculations, you will need to remember the following:

CLICKME\text{1}~CLICKME\text{dm^{3}}=CLICKME\text{1}~CLICKME\text{L}=CLICKME\text{1000}~CLICKME\text{mL}=CLICKME\text{1000}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}}, therefore dividing a volume in CLICKME\text{cm^{3}} by CLICKME\text{1000} will give you the equivalent volume in CLICKME\text{dm^{3}}.

Concentration calculations

How much sodium chloride (in g) will one need to prepare CLICKME\text{500}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}} of a standard solution with a concentration of CLICKME\text{0,01}~CLICKME\text{mol·dm^{-3}}?

Convert all quantities into the correct units for this equation.

CLICKME\begin{align*} C &= \frac{n}{V} \\ \text{0,01} & = \frac{n}{\text{0,5}} \\ n & = \text{0,005}\text{ mol} \end{align*}

Convert moles of CLICKME\text{NaCl} to mass.

CLICKME\begin{align*} m & = nM \\ & = (\text{0,005})(\text{58})\\ & = \text{0,29}\text{ g} \end{align*}

The mass of sodium chloride needed is CLICKME\text{0,29}~CLICKME\text{g}

We will now look at another use of concentration which is for titration calculations.

Titrations

A titration is a technique for determining the concentration of an unknown solution. Titrations can be done using many different types of reactions. Acid-base reactions and redox reactions are both commonly used for titrations.

In grade 10 you did a simple acid-base titration. Now we will look at how to calculate the concentration of an unknown solution using an acid-base titration.

When performing a titration we say that the substance of unknown concentration is titrated with the standard solution. A pipette is a measuring device that is used to measure an exact amount of a liquid. If you use a pipette to add liquid to a flask then you would say that the liquid was pipetted into a flask.

We can reduce the number of calculations that we have to do in titration calculations by using the following: CLICKME\begin{align*} \frac{C_{A}V_{A}}{a} & = \frac{C_{B}V_{B}}{b} \end{align*}

The CLICKMEa and CLICKMEb are the stoichiometric coefficients of compounds A and B respectively.

Titration calculation

Given the equation:

CLICKME\textrm{NaOH (aq)} + \textrm{HCl (aq)} → \textrm{NaCl (aq)} + \textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O (l)}

CLICKME\text{25}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}} of a CLICKME\text{0,2}~CLICKME\text{mol·dm^{-3}} hydrochloric acid solution was pipetted into a conical flask and titrated with sodium hydroxide. It was found that CLICKME\text{15}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}} of the sodium hydroxide was needed to neutralise the acid. Calculate the concentration of the sodium hydroxide.

Write down all the information you know about the reaction, and make sure that the equation is balanced.

CLICKME\text{NaOH}: CLICKMEV =\text{15}\text{ cm^{3}}

CLICKME\text{HCl}: CLICKMEV=\text{25}\text{ cm^{3}}; CLICKMEC= \text{0,2}\text{ mol·dm^{-3}}

The equation is already balanced.

Convert the volume to CLICKME\text{dm^{3}}
CLICKME\begin{align*} V_{\textrm{NaOH}} & = \frac{\text{15}}{\text{1000}} \\ & = \text{0,015}\text{ dm^{3}} \end{align*}CLICKME\begin{align*} V_{\textrm{HCl}} & = \frac{\text{25}}{\text{1000}} \\ & = \text{0,025}\text{ dm^{3}} \end{align*}
Calculate the concentration of the sodium hydroxide

CLICKME\begin{align*} \frac{C_{A}V_{A}}{a} & = \frac{C_{B}V_{B}}{b} \\ \frac{(\text{0,2})(\text{0,025})}{\text{1}} & = \frac{(C_{\textrm{NaOH}})(\text{0,015})}{\text{1}} \\ \text{0,005} & = (\text{0,015})C_{\textrm{NaOH}}\\ C_{\textrm{NaOH}} & = \text{0,33}\text{ mol·dm^{-3}} \end{align*}

The concentration of the CLICKME\text{NaOH} solution is CLICKME\text{0,33}~CLICKME\text{mol·dm^{-3}}

Titration calculation

CLICKME\text{4,9}~CLICKME\text{g} of sulfuric acid is dissolved in water and the final solution has a volume of CLICKME\text{220}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}}. Using an acid-base titration, it was found that CLICKME\text{20}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}} of this solution was able to completely neutralise CLICKME\text{10}~CLICKME\text{cm^{3}} of a sodium hydroxide solution. Calculate the concentration of the sodium hydroxide in CLICKME\text{mol·dm^{-3}}.

CLICKME\begin{align*} V & = \frac{\text{220}}{\text{1000}} = \text{0,22}\text{ dm^{3}} \end{align*}
