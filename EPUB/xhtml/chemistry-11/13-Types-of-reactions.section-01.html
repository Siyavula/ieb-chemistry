Most acids share certain characteristics, and most bases also share similar characteristics. It is important to be able to have a definition for acids and bases so that they can be correctly identified in reactions.

Defining acids and bases

One of the first things that was noted about acids is that they have a sour taste. Bases were noted to have a soapy feel and a bitter taste. However you cannot go around tasting and feeling unknown substances since they may be harmful. Also when chemists started to write down chemical reactions more practical definitions were needed.

A number of definitions for acids and bases have developed over the years. One of the earliest was the Arrhenius definition. Arrhenius (1887) noticed that water dissociates (splits up) into hydronium CLICKME(\text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+}) and hydroxide CLICKME(\text{OH}^{-}) ions according to the following equation:

CLICKME2\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O (l)} \rightarrow \textrm{H}_{3}\textrm{O}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{OH}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

For more information on dissociation, refer to Grade 10 (chapter 18: reactions in aqueous solution).

Arrhenius described an acid as a compound that increases the concentration of CLICKME\text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+} ions in solution and a base as a compound that increases the concentration of CLICKME\text{OH}^{-} ions in solution.

Look at the following examples showing the dissociation of hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide (a base) respectively:

  1. CLICKME\textrm{HCl (aq)} + \textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O}\textrm{(l)} → \textrm{H}_{3}\textrm{O}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{Cl}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    Hydrochloric acid in water increases the concentration of CLICKME\text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+} ions and is therefore an acid.

  2. CLICKME\textrm{NaOH (s)} \stackrel{\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O}}{\longrightarrow} \textrm{Na}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{OH}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    Sodium hydroxide in water increases the concentration of CLICKME\text{OH}^{-} ions and is therefore a base.

    Note that we write CLICKME\stackrel{\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O}}{\longrightarrow} to indicate that water is needed for the dissociation.

However, this definition could only be used for acids and bases in water. Since there are many reactions which do not occur in water it was important to come up with a much broader definition for acids and bases.

In 1923, Lowry and Bronsted took the work of Arrhenius further to develop a broader definition for acids and bases. The Bronsted-Lowry model defines acids and bases in terms of their ability to donate or accept protons.

Acids

A Bronsted-Lowry acid is a substance that gives away protons (hydrogen cations CLICKME\text{H}^{+}), and is therefore called a proton donor.

Bases

A Bronsted-Lowry base is a substance that takes up protons (hydrogen cations CLICKME\text{H}^{+}), and is therefore called a proton acceptor.

Below are some examples:

  1. CLICKME\textrm{HCl (aq)} + \textrm{NH}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{NH}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{Cl}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    We highlight the chlorine and the nitrogen so that we can follow what happens to these two elements as they react. We do not highlight the hydrogen atoms as we are interested in how these change. This colour coding is simply to help you identify the parts of the reaction and does not represent any specific property of these elements.

    CLICKME\textrm{H\textcolor{red}{Cl} (aq)} + \textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{\textcolor{red}{Cl}}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    In order to decide which substance is a proton donor and which is a proton acceptor, we need to look at what happens to each reactant. The reaction can be broken down as follows:

    CLICKME\textrm{H\textcolor{red}{Cl} (aq)} → \textrm{\textcolor{red}{Cl}}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} and

    CLICKME\textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)}

    From these reactions, it is clear that CLICKME\text{HCl} is a proton donor and is therefore an acid, and that CLICKME\text{NH}_{3} is a proton acceptor and is therefore a base.

  2. CLICKME\textrm{CH}_{3}\textrm{COOH (aq)} + \textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O (l)} → \textrm{H}_{3}\textrm{O}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{CH}_{3}\textrm{COO}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    Again we highlight the parts of the reactants that we want to follow in this reaction:

    CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textrm{CH}_{3}\textrm{COO}}\textrm{H (aq)} + \textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{\textcolor{blue}{O} (l)} → \textrm{H}_{3}\textrm{\textcolor{blue}{O}}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textcolor{red}{\textrm{CH}_{3}\textrm{COO}}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    The reaction can be broken down as follows:

    CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textrm{CH}_{3}\textrm{COO}}\textrm{H (aq)} → \textcolor{red}{\textrm{CH}_{3}\textrm{COO}}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} and

    CLICKME\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{\textcolor{blue}{O} (l)} → \textrm{H}_{3}\textrm{\textcolor{blue}{O}}^{+}\textrm{(aq)}

    In this reaction, CLICKME\text{CH}_{3}\text{COOH} (acetic acid or vinegar) is a proton donor and is therefore the acid. In this case, water acts as a base because it accepts a proton to form CLICKME\text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+}.

  3. CLICKME\textrm{NH}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O (l)} → \textrm{NH}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{OH}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    Again we highlight the parts of the reactants that we want to follow in this reaction:

    CLICKME\textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{\textcolor{red}{O} (l)} → \textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{\textcolor{red}{O}H}^{-}\textrm{(aq)}

    The reaction can be broken down as follows:

    CLICKME\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{\textcolor{red}{O} (l)} → \textrm{\textcolor{red}{O}H}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} and

    CLICKME\textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{\textcolor{blue}{N}H}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)}

    Water donates a proton and is therefore an acid in this reaction. Ammonia accepts the proton and is therefore the base.

Notice in these examples how we looked at the common elements to break the reaction into two parts. So in the first example we followed what happened to chlorine to see if it was part of the acid or the base. And we also followed nitrogen to see if it was part of the acid or the base. You should also notice how in the reaction for the acid there is one less hydrogen on the right hand side and in the reaction for the base there is an extra hydrogen on the right hand side.

Amphoteric substances

In examples CLICKME\text{2} and CLICKME\text{3} above we notice an interesting thing about water. In example CLICKME\text{2} we find that water acts as a base (it accepts a proton). In example CLICKME\text{3} however we see that water acts as an acid (it donates a proton)!

Depending on what water is reacting with it can either react as a base or as an acid. Water is said to be amphoteric. Water is not unique in this respect, several other substances are also amphoteric.

Amphoteric

An amphoteric substance is one that can react as either an acid or base.

When we look just at Bronsted-Lowry acids and bases we can also talk about amphiprotic substances which are a special type of amphoteric substances.

Amphiprotic

An amphiprotic substance is one that can react as either a proton donor (Bronsted-Lowry acid) or as a proton acceptor (Bronsted-Lowry base). Examples of amphiprotic substances include water, hydrogen carbonate ion (CLICKME\text{HCO}_{3}^{-}) and hydrogen sulfate ion (CLICKME\text{HSO}_{4}^{-}).

Note: You may also see the term ampholyte used to mean a substance that can act as both an acid and a base. This term is no longer in general use in chemistry.

Polyprotic acids [NOT IN CAPS]

A polyprotic (many protons) acid is an acid that has more than one proton that it can donate. For example sulfuric acid can donate one proton to form the hydrogen sulfate ion:

CLICKME\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{SO}_{4}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{OH}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} \rightarrow \textrm{HSO}_{4}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} + \textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O (l)}

Or it can donate two protons to form the sulfate ion:

CLICKME\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{SO}_{4}\textrm{(aq)} + 2\textrm{OH}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} \rightarrow \textrm{SO}_{4}^{2-}\textrm{(aq)} + 2\textrm{H}_{2}\textrm{O (l)}

In this chapter we will mostly consider monoprotic acids (acids with only one proton to donate). If you do see a polyprotic acid in a reaction then write the resulting reaction equation with the acid donating all its protons.

Some examples of polyprotic acids are: CLICKME\text{H}_{2}\text{SO}_{4}, CLICKME\text{H}_{2}\text{SO}_{3}, CLICKME\text{H}_{2}\text{CO}_{3} and CLICKME\text{H}_{3}\text{PO}_{4}.

Conjugate acid-base pairs

Look at the reaction between hydrochloric acid and ammonia to form ammonium and chloride ions (again we have highlighted the different parts of the equation):

CLICKME\textrm{\textcolor{red}{HCl} (aq)} + \textcolor{blue}{\textrm{NH}_{3}}\textrm{(aq)} → \textcolor{blue}{\textrm{NH}_{4}^{+}}\textrm{(aq)} + \textcolor{red}{\textrm{Cl}^{-}}\textrm{(aq)}

We look at what happens to each of the reactants in the reaction:

CLICKME\textrm{HCl (aq)} → \textrm{Cl}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} and

CLICKME\textrm{NH}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{NH}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)}

We see that CLICKME\text{HCl} acts as the acid and CLICKME\text{NH}_{3} acts as the base.

But what if we actually had the following reaction:

CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textrm{NH}_{4}^{+}}\textrm{(aq)} + \textcolor{red}{\textrm{Cl}^{-}}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{\textcolor{red}{HCl} (aq)} + \textcolor{blue}{\textrm{NH}_{3}}\textrm{(aq)}

This is the same reaction as the first one, but the products are now the reactants.

Now if we look at the what happens to each of the reactants we see the following:

CLICKME\textrm{NH}_{4}^{+}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{NH}_{3}\textrm{(aq)} and

CLICKME\textrm{Cl}^{-}\textrm{(aq)} → \textrm{HCl (aq)}

We see that CLICKME\text{NH}_{4}^{+} acts as the acid and CLICKME\text{Cl}^{-} acts as the base.

Up to now you have looked at reactions as starting with the reactants and going to the products. For acids and bases we also need to consider what happens if we swop the reactants and the products around. This will help you understand conjugate acid-base pairs.

When CLICKME\text{HCl} (the acid) loses a proton it forms CLICKME\text{Cl}^{-} (the base). And that when CLICKME\text{Cl}^{-} (the base) gains a proton it forms CLICKME\text{HCl} (the acid). We call these two species a conjugate acid-base pair. Similarly CLICKME\text{NH}_{3} and CLICKME\text{NH}_{4}^{+} form a conjugate acid-base pair.

The word conjugate means coupled or connected.

In your own words explain what is meant by the term conjugate acid-base pair.

A conjugate acid-base pair is a reactant and product pair that is transformed into each other through the loss or gain of a proton. So for example an acid loses a proton to form a base. The acid and the resulting base are said to be a conjugate acid-base pair.
