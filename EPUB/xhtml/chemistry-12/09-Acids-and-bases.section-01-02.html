Models for acids and bases

For the acids you will encounter this year, an acid is a molecule that donates a H + \text{H}^{+} ion.

Substances that will act as a base include hydroxides, oxides, carbonates or hydrogen carbonate, among others. Bases often release free hydroxide ions ( OH − \text{OH}^{-}) when dissociating in water.

Arrhenius model for acids and bases

A number of models for acids and bases have been developed over the years. One of the earliest was the Arrhenius definition. In 1884, Arrhenius discovered that water dissociates (splits up) into hydronium ( H 3 O + ) (\text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+}) and hydroxide ( OH − ) (\text{OH}^{-}) ions according to the following equation:

2 H 2 O ( ℓ ) 2\text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{ℓ}) ⇋ H 3 O + (aq) + OH − (aq) ⇋ {\color{red}{\text{H}_{3}{\text{O}}^{+}{\text{(aq)}}}} + {\color{blue}{\text{OH}^{-}{\text{(aq)}}}}

Another way of writing this is:

H 2 O ( ℓ ) \text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{ℓ}) ⇋ H + (aq) + OH − (aq) ⇋ {\color{red}{\text{H}^{+}{\text{(aq)}}}} + {\color{blue}{\text{OH}^{-}{\text{(aq)}}}}

Arrhenius described an acid as a compound that forms H 3 O + \text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+} when added to water. An Arrhenius acid therefore increases the concentration of H 3 O + \text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+} ions ( [ H 3 O + \text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+}] ) in water. Arrhenius described a base as a compound that dissociates in water to form OH − \text{OH}^{-} ions. An Arrhenius base therefore increases the concentration of OH − \text{OH}^{-} ions ([ OH − \text{OH}^{-}] ) in water.

Arrhenius acids and bases

An Arrhenius acid forms H 3 O + \text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+} in water (increases [ H 3 O + \text{H}_{3}\text{O}^{+}]). An Arrhenius base forms OH − \text{OH}^{-} in water (increases [ OH − \text{OH}^{-}]).

Look at the following examples showing the dissociation of hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide:

  1. HCl(aq) {\color{red}{\text{HCl(aq)}}} + H 2 O ( ℓ ) \text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{ℓ}) → H 3 O + (aq) \to {\color{red}{\text{H}_{3}{\text{O}}^{+}{\text{(aq)}}}} + Cl − ( aq ) \text{Cl}^{-}(\text{aq})

    Hydrochloric acid {\color{red}{\text{Hydrochloric acid}}} in water increases the concentration of H 3 O + {\color{red}{\text{H}_{3}{\text{O}}^{+}}} ions and is therefore an acid {\color{red}{\textit{acid}}}.

  2. NaOH(s) {\color{blue}{\text{NaOH(s)}}} + H 2 O ( ℓ ) \text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{ℓ}) → \to Na + ( aq ) \text{Na}^{+}(\text{aq}) + OH − (aq) {\color{blue}{\text{OH}^{-}{\text{(aq)}}}} + H 2 O ( ℓ ) \text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{ℓ})

    Sodium hydroxide {\color{blue}{\text{Sodium hydroxide}}} in water increases the concentration of OH − {\color{blue}{\text{OH}^{-}}} ions and is therefore a base {\color{blue}{\textit{base}}}.

However, this definition can only be used for acids and bases in water. Since there are many reactions which do not occur in water, it was important to come up with a much broader definition for acids and bases.

Brønsted-Lowry model for acids and bases

In 1923, Lowry and Brønsted took the work of Arrhenius further to develop a broader definition for acids and bases. The Brønsted-Lowry model defines acids and bases in terms of their ability to donate or accept protons (Table tab-acidsandbases-bronstedlowry).

Hydrogen atoms contain only one proton. H + \text{H}^{+} is a hydrogen atom that has lost its electron and is often called a proton.

Brønsted-Lowry acids and bases

An acid is a substance that donates (gives away) protons ( H + \text{H}^{+}). A base is a substance that accepts (takes) protons.

Under the Brønsted-Lowry definition: an acid \color{red}{\text{acid}} is a proton donor \color{red}{\textbf{proton donor}}; a base \color{blue}{\text{base}} is a proton \color{blue}{\textbf{proton}}  acceptor \color{blue}{\textbf{ acceptor}}.

Water can act as both an acid and a base depending on the reaction. A substance that can act as either an acid or a base is called amphoteric.

Amphoteric

An amphoteric substance is one that can act as an acid in one reaction, or a base in another reaction.

An amphoteric substance that contains both acidic and basic functional groups is called a ampholyte.

An amphiprotic substance is a amphoteric substance that can donate a proton in one reaction (a Brønsted-Lowry acid), or accept a proton in another reaction (a Brønsted-Lowry base).

Amphiprotic

An amphiprotic substance can donate a proton in one reaction, or accept a proton in another reaction.

Substances such as ammonia ( NH 3 \text{NH}_{3}), zinc oxide ( ZnO \text{ZnO}), and beryllium hydroxide ( Be ( OH ) 2 \text{Be}(\text{OH})_{2}) are amphoteric. Water and ammonia are also amphiprotic.

An acid that releases only one \textcolor d a r k g r e e n H + {\textcolor{darkgreen}{\text{H}^{+}}} ion per molecule of acid (e.g. \textcolor d a r k g r e e n H \textcolor{darkgreen}{\text{H}}Cl) is referred to as monoprotic. An acid that can release two \textcolor d a r k g r e e n H + {\textcolor{darkgreen}{\text{H}^{+}}} ions per molecule of acid (e.g. \textcolor d a r k g r e e n H 2 \textcolor{darkgreen}{\text{H}_{2}} SO 4 \text{SO}_{4}) is referred to as diprotic. Any acid than can donate more than one \textcolor d a r k g r e e n H + {\textcolor{darkgreen}{ \text{H}^{+}}} ion per molecule of acid can be referred to as polyprotic (this means that diprotic acids are also polyprotic).
