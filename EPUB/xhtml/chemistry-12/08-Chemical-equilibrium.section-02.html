The equilibrium constant

Consider the reversible chemical reaction:

a \textcolor o r a n g e A + b \textcolor o r a n g e B ⇌ c \textcolor p u r p l e C + d \textcolor p u r p l e D {\text{a}}\textcolor{orange}{\text{A}} + {\text{b}}\textcolor{orange}{\text{B}} ⇌ {\text{c}}\textcolor{purple}{\text{C}} + {\text{d}}\textcolor{purple}{\text{D}}

\textcolor o r a n g e A \textcolor{orange}{\text{A}} and \textcolor o r a n g e B \textcolor{orange}{\text{B}} are the reactants, \textcolor p u r p l e C \textcolor{purple}{\text{C}} and \textcolor p u r p l e D \textcolor{purple}{\text{D}} are the products and a, b, c, and d are the coefficients from the balanced reaction.

When the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction, the system is in chemical equilibrium. It is useful to know how much of each substance is in the container - in particular the amount of products compared to the amount of reactants. A simple ratio from the balanced chemical equation gives us a number called the equilibrium constant (\(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\)).

The subscript c refers to the concentration of all the substances at equilibrium.

K c = [\textcolor{purple}{\text{C}}\text] c [\textcolor{purple}{\text{D}}\text] d [\textcolor{orange}{\text{A}}\text] a [\textcolor{orange}{\text{B}}\text] b \text{K}_{\text{c}} = \dfrac{{\text{[\textcolor{purple}{\text{C}}\text]}}^{c}{\text{[\textcolor{purple}{\text{D}}\text]}}^{d}}{{\text{[\textcolor{orange}{\text{A}}\text]}}^{a}{\text{[\textcolor{orange}{\text{B}}\text]}}^{b}}

The equation for the equilibrium constant uses the concentrations of the reactants and products. Square brackets mean the concentration of the compound written within the square brackets: [x] means the concentration of x.

[ \textcolor o r a n g e A ] , [ \textcolor o r a n g e B ] , [ \textcolor p u r p l e C ] and [ \textcolor p u r p l e D ] [\textcolor{orange}{\text{A}}\text], [\textcolor{orange}{\text{B}}\text], [\textcolor{purple}{\text{C}}\text] \text{and} [\textcolor{purple}{\text{D}}\text] are the molar concentrations of each substance present at equilibrium.

The equilibrium constant

The equilibrium constant (\(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\)) is the ratio between the concentration of products and reactants in a chemical reaction.

When the concentration of the reactants is much larger than the concentration of the products \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) will be small (normally less than one). When the concentration of the reactants is much less than that of the products \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) will be large (normally greater than one).

For example:

2 \textcolor o r a n g e NO (g) + 2 \textcolor o r a n g e H 2 (g) ⇌ \textcolor p u r p l e N 2 (g) + 2 \textcolor p u r p l e H 2 O(g) 2\textcolor{orange}{\text{NO}{\text{(g)}}} + 2\textcolor{orange}{\text{H}_{2}{\text{(g)}}} \rightleftharpoons \textcolor{purple}{\text{N}_{2}{\text{(g)}}} + 2\textcolor{purple}{\text{H}_{2}{\text{O(g)}}}

The \textcolor o r a n g e reactants \textcolor{orange}{\textbf{reactants}} are \textcolor o r a n g e NO \textcolor{orange}{\text{NO}} and \textcolor o r a n g e H 2 \textcolor{orange}{\text{H}_{2}}. The \textcolor p u r p l e products \textcolor{purple}{\textbf{products}} are \textcolor p u r p l e N 2 \textcolor{purple}{\text{N}_{2}} and \textcolor p u r p l e H 2 O \textcolor{purple}{\text{H}_{2}{\text{O}}}.

It is important to look at the coefficients of the equation as well:

\textcolor o r a n g e 2 \textcolor{orange}{\textbf{2}}\(\text{NO}(\text{g})\) + \textcolor o r a n g e 2 \textcolor{orange}{\textbf{2}}\(\text{H}_{2}(\text{g})\) ⇌ \textcolor p u r p l e 1 \rightleftharpoons \textcolor{purple}{\textbf{1}}\(\text{N}_{2}(\text{g})\) + \textcolor p u r p l e 2 \textcolor{purple}{\textbf{2}}\(\text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{g})\)

\(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) for this equation will therefore be written as follows:

K c = [N 2 ] \textcolor p u r p l e 1 [H 2 O] \textcolor p u r p l e 2 [NO] \textcolor o r a n g e 2 [H 2 ] \textcolor o r a n g e 2 \text{K}_{\text{c}}=\dfrac{\text{[N}_{2}{\text{]}}{\textcolor{purple}{^{1}}}{\text{[H}}_{2}{\text{O]}}{\textcolor{purple}{^{2}}}}{\text{[NO]}{\textcolor{orange}{^{2}}}{\text{[H}}_{2}{\text{]}}{\textcolor{orange}{^{2}}}}

In the expression for \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) the concentration of a product or reactant is taken to the power of its coefficient in the balanced reaction. So if the coefficient of a product (C) is 3 in the balanced equation, then the concentration of C ([C]) will be written \([\text{C}]^{3}\) in the expression for \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\).

We leave out reactants or products that are either pure liquids or in the solid phase when calculating \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\). For example:

\textcolor r e d C(s) \textcolor{red}{\text{C(s)}} \(+ \text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{g})\) ⇌ ⇌ \(\text{CO}(\text{g}) + \text{H}_{2}(\text{g})\) \rule 40. p t 0 e x K c = [CO][H 2 ] [H 2 O] {\phantom{\rule{40.pt}{0ex}}}\text{K}_{\text{c}}=\dfrac{{\text{[CO][H}}_{2}\text{]}}{{\text{[H}}_{2}\text{O]}}

Calculating the equilibrium constant

When calculations involving the equilibrium constant are done, the following tips may help:

  1. Always read the question carefully to be sure that you understand what you have been asked to calculate.

  2. If the equilibrium constant is involved, make sure that the concentrations you use are the concentrations at equilibrium, and not the concentrations or quantities that are present at some other time in the reaction.

  3. When you are doing more complicated calculations, it helps to draw up a RICE table (Table tab-chemicalequilibrium-RICEtable). This is gone into in more detail later in this section.

Remember that the general form of the expression for \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) of a reaction at chemical equilibrium (a \textcolor o r a n g e A \textcolor{orange}{\text{A}} + b \textcolor o r a n g e B \textcolor{orange}{\text{B}} ⇌ ⇌ c \textcolor p u r p l e C \textcolor{purple}{\text{C}} + d \textcolor p u r p l e D \textcolor{purple}{\text{D}}) is:

K c = [\textcolor{purple}{\text{C}}\text] c [\textcolor{purple}{\text{D}}\text] d [\textcolor{orange}{\text{A}}\text] a [\textcolor{orange}{\text{B}}\text] b {K}_{c}=\dfrac{{\text{[\textcolor{purple}{\text{C}}\text]}}^{c}{\text{[\textcolor{purple}{\text{D}}\text]}}^{d}}{{\text{[\textcolor{orange}{\text{A}}\text]}}^{a}{\text{[\textcolor{orange}{\text{B}}\text]}}^{b}}

The meaning of \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) values

The formula for \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) has:

  • the concentration of the products in the numerator and the concentration of reactants in the denominator

  • a \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) value \textcolor r e d greater than 1 \textcolor{red}{\textbf{greater than 1}} means that the equilibrium lies to the right

    \textcolor r e d there are more products than reactants \textcolor{red}{\textbf{there are more products than reactants}}

  • a \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) value \textcolor b l u e between 0 and 1 \textcolor{blue}{\textbf{between 0 and 1}} means that the equilibrium lies to the left

    \textcolor b l u e more reactants than products \textcolor{blue}{\textbf{more reactants than products}}

This has been summarised in Table fig-tab-Kcval. Note that \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) cannot be 0 or less.

Yield describes the quantity of product in the container relative to the maximum product possible.

A high \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) value means that:

  • the concentration of products is high

  • the equilibrium lies far to the right

  • the reaction has a high yield

A low \(\text{K}_{\text{c}}\) value (close to 0) means that:

  • the concentration of reactants is high

  • the equilibrium lies far to the left

  • the reaction has a low yield
