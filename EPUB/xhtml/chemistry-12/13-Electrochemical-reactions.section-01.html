Oxidation and reduction

You should remember the terms oxidation and reduction from Grade 11:

  • CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{Oxidation}} involves a loss of electrons

  • CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{Reduction}} involves a gain of electrons.

An easy way to remember this is:

In both of these processes a transfer of electrons is involved resulting in a change in the oxidation state of the elements.

An element or compound that releases electrons is CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{oxidised}}. As it accepts electrons it takes them away from another element or compound and the element or compound it takes them from is CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{oxidised}}. In electrochemical cells there are two electrodes - an anode and a cathode.

CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\text{Oxidation}} always occurs at the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode}} while CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\text{reduction}} always occurs at the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{cathode}}. In this chapter, we will be looking more closely at two different types of electrochemical cells: galvanic and electrolytic cells. Before we go into detail on galvanic and electrolytic cells you'll need to know a few definitions:

Electrode

An electrode is an electrical conductor that connects the electrochemical species from its solution to the external electrical circuit of the cell.

Electrolyte

An electrolyte is a solution that contains free ions, and which therefore behaves as a conductor of charges (electrical conductor) in solution.

Salt bridge

A salt bridge is a material which contains electrolytic solution and acts as a connection between two half-cells (completes the circuit). It maintains electrical neutrality in and between the electrolytes in the half-cell compartments.

Galvanic cells

A galvanic cell (which is also sometimes referred to as a voltaic cell) consists of two half-cells, which convert chemical potential energy into electrical energy.

Galvanic cell

A galvanic (voltaic) cell is an electrochemical cell that uses a (spontaneous) chemical reaction between two different electrodes, dipped in an electrolyte, to generate a chemical potential difference.

Let the metal at the anode be X. Then the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode half-reaction}} is:

CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{{\textbf{X(s)}} \to {\textbf{X}}^{+}{\textbf{(aq) + e}}^{-}}

This half-reaction occurs in the half-cell containing the X(s) anode and the CLICKME\text{X}^{+}(\text{aq}) electrolyte solution.

If the metal at the cathode is Y then the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{cathode half-reaction}} is:

CLICKME\textcolor{red}{{\textbf{Y}}^{+}{\textbf{(aq) + e}}^{-} \to {\textbf{Y(s)}}}

This means that the overall reaction is: CLICKME\text{X}(\text{s}) + \text{Y}^{+}(\text{aq}) CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{X}^{+}(\text{aq}) + \text{Y}(\text{s}).

This half-reaction occurs in the half-cell containing the Y(s) cathode and the CLICKME\text{Y}^{+}(\text{aq}) electrolyte solution.

To represent this reaction using standard cell notation we write the following:

CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{X(s)}}CLICKME|CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{X}^{+}{\textbf{(aq)}}}CLICKME||CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{Y}^{+}{\textbf{(aq)}}}CLICKME|CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{Y(s)}}

By convention, the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode}} is always written on the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{left}} and the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{cathode}} is always written on the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{right}}. These are divided by CLICKME|| representing the salt bridge. The different phases within each half-cell (solid (s) and aqueous (aq) here) are separated by CLICKME|.

In each half-cell there is a metal rod (electrode) and these are connected through a wire in the external circuit. There is also a salt bridge between the individual half-cells. A galvanic cell uses the reactions that take place at these two metals to produce electrical energy, i.e. the reaction occurs without the need to add energy.

A spontaneous reaction is one that will occur without the need for external energy. Refer to the subsection on spontaneity for more information.

In a galvanic cell:

  • electrons are deposited on the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode}} as the metal is CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{oxidised}} to form metal ions

  • at the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{cathode}} metal ions in the solution are being CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{reduced}} (accepting electrons) and depositing on the metal

  • therefore there are more electrons at the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode}} than at the cathode

  • movement is always from areas of high concentration to low concentration, therefore the electrons move CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{from the anode}}, through the external circuit, CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{to the}}CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{cathode}}

  • conventional current is in the opposite direction (from the cathode to the anode)

  • the electrolyte solution consists of metal cations and counter anions

  • as the negatively charged electrons are flowing from the anode to the cathode, the negatively charged anions must flow CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{from the cathode}} half-cell CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{to the anode}} half-cell

  • the postive metal ions forming around the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode}} must flow CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{from the anode}} half-cell CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{to the cathode}} half-cell.

  • this movement of ions occurs through the salt bridge, which allows a balancing of the charges in the solutions and allows the electrochemical reaction in the cell to continue

Conclusions

When a zinc sulfate solution containing a zinc plate is connected by a U-tube to a copper(II) sulfate solution containing a copper plate, reactions occur in both solutions. The decrease in mass of the zinc plate suggests that the zinc metal electrode has been oxidised. The increase in mass of the copper plate suggests that reduction of CLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+} ions has occurred here to produce more copper metal.

The important thing to notice in this experiment is that:

  • the chemical reactions that take place at the two electrodes cause an electric current to flow through the external circuit

  • the reaction must be a spontaneous redox reaction

  • chemical energy is converted to electrical energy

  • the zinc-copper cell is one example of a galvanic cell.

It was the Italian physician and anatomist Luigi Galvani who marked the birth of electrochemistry by making a link between chemical reactions and electricity. In 1780, Galvani discovered that when two different metals (copper and zinc for example) were connected to each other and then both touched to different parts of a nerve of a frog leg at the same time, they made the leg contract. He called this 'animal electricity'.

In the zinc-copper cell, the copper and zinc plates are called the electrodes. The U-tube (salt bridge) also plays a very important role in the cell:

  • In the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{zinc half-cell}} (CLICKME\text{Zn}(\text{s})|\text{Zn}^{2+}(\text{aq})), there is a build-up of positive charge because of the release of electrons (onto the anode) through CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{oxidation}}.

  • In the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{copper half-cell}} (CLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+}(\text{aq})|\text{Cu}(\text{s})), there is a decrease in the positive charge because electrons are gained (from the cathode) through CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{reduction}}.

  • This causes a movement of CLICKME\text{SO}_{4}^{2-}(\text{aq}) ions into the beaker where there are too many positive ions, in order to neutralise the solution. That is, from the copper half-cell to the zinc half-cell.

  • The salt bridge acts as a transfer medium that allows ions to flow through without allowing the different solutions to mix and react.

  • Without the salt bridge, the flow of electrons in the outer circuit stops completely. This is because the salt bridge is needed to complete the circuit.

Electrolytic cells

Is it possible to use an electric current to force a particular chemical reaction to occur, which would otherwise not take place? The answer is yes, and the type of cell that is used to do this, is called an electrolytic cell.

Electrolytic cell

An electrolytic cell is a type of cell that uses electricity to drive a non-spontaneous reaction.

An electrolytic cell is activated by applying an electrical potential across the electrodes to force an internal chemical reaction between the ions that are in the electrolyte solution. This process is called electrolysis.

Electrolysis

Electrolysis is a method of separating bonded elements and compounds by passing an electric current through them.

In an electrolytic cell:

  • an electrode is connected to the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{positive terminal}} of the battery and becomes the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode}}

  • at the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{positive anode}} metal atoms are CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{oxidised}} to form metal ions and move into solution, leaving their electrons on the electrode

  • an electrode is connected to the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{negative terminal}} of the battery and becomes the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{cathode}}

  • metal cations in the electrolyte solution are attracted to the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{negative cathode}} and are CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{reduced}} to form metal ions

As there are no half-cells in an electrolytic cell the electrolyte solution consists of metal cations and counter anions, which can move freely so long as the cell is connected to an external power source.

The electrodes in an electrolytic cell can be the same metal. Let that metal be Z.

Then the CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{anode half-reaction}} is:

CLICKME\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{Z(s)} \to {\textbf{Z}}^{+}{\textbf{(aq)}} + {\textbf{e}}^{-}}

And the CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{cathode half-reaction}} is:

CLICKME\textcolor{red}{\textbf{Z}^{+}{\textbf{(aq)}} + {\textbf{e}}^{-} \to {\textbf{Z(s)}}}

This means that the overall reaction is: CLICKME\text{Z}(\text{s}) + \text{Z}^{+}(\text{aq})CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{Z}^{+}(\text{aq}) + \text{Z}(\text{s}).

While this might seem trivial this is an important technique to purify metals.

Observations

  • The initial blue colour of the solution remains unchanged throughout the experiment.

  • It appears that copper has been deposited on one of the electrodes (it increased in mass) but dissolved from the other (it decreased in mass).

  • The electrode connected to the negative terminal of the battery will have a negative charge. The electrode connected to the positive terminal of the battery will have a positive charge.

  • Positively charged CLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+} ions are attracted to the negatively charged electrode. These ions gain electrons and are reduced to form copper metal, which is deposited on the electrode. The half-reaction that takes place is as follows:

    CLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+}(\text{aq}) + 2\text{e}^{-}CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{Cu}(\text{s}) (reduction half-reaction)

    Reduction occurs at the cathode. Therefore, the electrode which had deposits is the cathode.

  • At the positive electrode, copper metal is oxidised to form CLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+} ions, leaving electrons on the electrode. This is why it appears that some of the copper has dissolved from the electrode. The half-reaction that takes place is as follows:

    CLICKME\text{Cu}(\text{s})CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+}(\text{aq}) + 2\text{e}^{-} (oxidation half-reaction)

    Oxidation is loss at the anode. Therefore, the electrode which appeared to have dissolved is the anode.

  • The amount of copper that is deposited at one electrode is approximately the same as the amount of copper that is dissolved from the other. The number of CLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+} ions in the solution therefore remains almost the same, and the blue colour of the solution is unchanged.

Conclusion

In this demonstration, an electric current was used to break-up CLICKME\text{CuSO}_{4} into its component ions, CLICKME\text{Cu}^{2+} and CLICKME\text{SO}_{4}^{2-}. This process is called electrolysis. Note that, unlike in the previous galvanic cell experiment, the cathode is negative and the anode is positive.

CLICKME2\text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{ℓ})CLICKME\toCLICKME2\text{H}_{2}(\text{g}) + \text{O}_{2}(\text{g})

Results

  • In the electrolysis of water two CLICKME\text{H}^{+} ions each gain an electron (are reduced) and combine to form hydrogen gas (CLICKME\text{H}_{2}(\text{g})):

    CLICKME2\text{H}^{+}(\text{aq}) + 2\text{e}^{-}CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{H}_{2}(\text{g})

    CLICKME\text{O}^{2-} loses two electrons (is oxidised) to form oxygen gas:

    CLICKME2\text{O}^{2-}(\text{aq})CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{O}_{2}(\text{g}) + 4\text{e}^{-}

  • The positive CLICKME\text{H}^{+} ions will be attracted to the negative electrode. Reduction is a gain of electrons at the cathode, therefore the negative electrode is the cathode.

  • The negative CLICKME\text{O}^{2-} ions will be attracted to the positive electrode. Oxidation is a loss of electrons at the anode, therefore the positive electrode is the anode.

  • Remember that pure water does not conduct electricity. So, an electrolyte (such as sulfuric acid) is necessary for the reaction to take place.

  • A salt dissolved in the water is also an electrolyte, so sulfuric acid is not necessary in the second beaker.

  • The electrolysis of water occurs in beaker 2, but there are other reactions taking place as well because of the presence of the CLICKME\text{Na}^{+} and CLICKME\text{I}^{-} ions.

  • The positive CLICKME\text{Na}^{+} ions will be attracted to the negative electrode:

    CLICKME\text{Na}^{+}(\text{aq}) + \text{e}^{-}CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{Na}(\text{s})

    CLICKME\text{Na}(\text{s}) is very reactive with water and will immediately undergo the following reaction:

    CLICKME2\text{Na}(\text{s}) + 2\text{H}_{2}\text{O}(\text{ℓ})CLICKME\toCLICKME2\text{NaOH}(\text{aq}) + \text{H}_{2}(\text{g}).

  • The negative CLICKME\text{I}^{-} ions will be attracted to the positive electrode:

    CLICKME2\text{I}^{-}(\text{aq})CLICKME\toCLICKME\text{I}_{2}(\text{s}) + 2\text{e}^{-}

    You should have observed solid iodine forming at the positive electrode.

  • Remember that phenolphthalein turns pink in the presence of a base. So when the phenolphthalein is added, the water around the negative electrode should become pink due to the CLICKME\text{NaOH}.

Conclusion

The application of an electric current to water splits the water molecules and causes hydrogen and oxygen gas to form. This can only happen in the presence of an electrolyte (for example sulfuric acid). Sodium iodide dissolved in the water is also an electrolyte and enables the electrolysis of water. However, the cation and anion of the salt will also undergo a reaction. CLICKME\text{Na}^{+} will form a base at the cathode, while solid iodine will form at the anode.
