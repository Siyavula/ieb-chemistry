Introduction

Matter is not created or destroyed in chemical reactions.  In any reaction all that happens is that atoms are rearranged in different, yet fixed ratios.   The mass gained or lost by chemicals within a reaction vessel are due to the movement of atoms into or out of the reaction vessel from the surroundings.  The total mass of all chemicals reacting in a reaction are equal to the total mass of all the products formed in a reaction.  In an experiment it is possible to weigh chemicals on an electronic scale to determine the number of atoms that are combining, and therefore the ratios involved in reactions.    Knowledge of the ratio of atoms within a compound provides information about the structure of the substances.  Once the formula of a compound is established it is possible to write balanced chemical equations for the reaction.  The balanced chemical reaction allows one to measure the appropriate amounts suitable for the reaction.   It is a bit like manufacturing cakes in a bakery.  Buying the ingredients in the ratio of the recipe will allow the baker to manufacture cakes so that no ingredients are in excess at the end of a week of baking cakes.

Learning Objectives


Balanced chemical equations

  • Know the name and formula of the following polyatomic ions: ammonium, chlorate, ethanoate, hydroxide, nitrate, nitrite, permanganate, hydrogen carbonate, hydrogen sulphate, carbonate, dichromate, sulphate, sulphite, phosphate.

  • Write chemical formulae using the periodic table and knowledge of polyatomic ions.

  • Represent chemical changes using balanced reaction equations i.e. translate word equations into chemical equations with formulae, using subscripts to represent phases (s), (l), (g) and (aq).

  • Balance reaction equations by inspection.

  • Interpret balanced reaction equations in terms of:

    • conservation of atoms

    • conservation of mass (use relative atomic masses)

The mole concept

The conservation of atoms in chemical reactions leads to the principle of conservation of matter and the ability to calculate the mass of products and reactants.

  • Describe the mole as the SI unit for amount of substance.

  • Relate amount of substance to relative atomic mass.

  • State that one mole contains Avogadro's number of particles ( N A \text{N}_{\text{A}} = 6,02 × 10 23 \text{6,02} \times \text{10}^{\text{23}}~ mol^{-1} \text{mol^{-1}}).

  • Define molar mass as as the mass in grams of one mole of that substance.

  • Calculate the molar mass of a substance given its formula.

  • Solve problems using the equation: n = m M \text{n}=\dfrac{\text{m}}{\text{M}}

    Where n = number of moles, m = mass of substance, and M = molar mass.

Molar volume of gases

  • Know and use the fact that 1 \text{1} mole of gas occupies 22,4 \text{22,4}~ dm^{3} \text{dm^{3}} at 0 \text{0} °C \text{°C} ( 273 \text{273}~ K \text{K}) and at 1 \text{1} atmosphere ( 101,3 \text{101,3}~ kPa \text{kPa}).

  • Solve problems using V = nV m \text{V} = \text{nV}_{\text{m}} where V m \text{V}_{\text{m}} = 22,4 \text{22,4}~ dm^{3} \text{dm^{3}} is the molar volume.

Concentration of solutions

  • Define solute as the substance that is dissolved in the solution.

  • Define solvent as the substance in which another substance is dissolved, forming a solution.

  • Identify the solute and/or solvent for a particular solution.

  • Define concentration as as the number of moles of solute per unit volume of solution.

  • Calculate molar concentration of a solution using the equation C = n V \text{C} = \dfrac{\text{n}}{\text{V}}

  • Define a standard solution as as a solution of known concentration.

  • Describe quantitatively and qualitatively how to make up a standard solution.

Stoichiometric calculations

  • Perform stoichiometric calculations using balanced equations (may include limiting reagents).

  • Perform stoichiometric calculations to determine the percentage yield of a chemical reaction.

Revision

  • A chemical formula represents the ratio of atoms in a compound or element.     Let us look at the formula for water, H2O.  We see that it contains two elements, hydrogen (H) and oxygen (O).  The subscript following the symbol H tells us that  two hydrogen atoms combined with one oxygen atom.  Consider the diagrams below of 3O2  and  2O3.  The 3 in front of O2 tells us that there are three separate unit of O2, while the 2 in front of O3 refers to two separate units of O3.

  • Giant structures have continuous bonding and don’t form simple molecules.  Due to this continuous bonding they are mostly solids at room temperature.  Ionic and metallic structures are giant and therefore have high melting and boiling points.
  • Molecular substances that have covalent bonding are mostly liquids or gases and have low melting point as solids.
  • A precipitate is a solid that forms in solution, often due to anions and cations bonding in an ionic structure.
  • Some elements occur naturally as molecules of two atoms.  These are known as diatomic molecules.  The following are the elements that form diatomic molecules: F2, Cl2, Br2, I2, H2, O2 and N2.

    Formulae by experimentation

    A chemical formula gives the symbols of the elements combined, with subscripts indicating the ratio of the atoms which are combined. A chemical formula can be determined theoretically using our understanding of electronic charge, but in almost all cases the formula has at some point been confirmed by experimentation.  When something is established through experimentation we say that it has been empirically established. The product formed by heating iron fillings in air has two possible correct formulae, either FeO or Fe2O3.  It is only by experimentation that we can confirm which one is correct.

The formula of iron oxide formed when heating iron filing

Aim

To determine the formula of iron oxide

Apparatus 

  • Bunsen burner, crucible, tripod, spatula, crucible tongs, heat proof surface.
  • Fine iron powder (Coarseness about the same as castor sugar)
  • Access to a two decimal placed scale

Diagram


Method

  • Weight the crucible in grams to two decimal places.
  • Add approximately 0,5 g of fine iron powder to the crucible and measure the mass in grams to two decimal places.
  • Heat the crucible with iron gently at first, followed by maximum heating for 5 minutes.
  • Remove the crucible using the tongs and allow to cool on the heat proof surface.
  • Measure the mass of the iron oxide and crucible.

Writing Formulae theoretically

Ionic compounds are not made up of separate molecules but are made up of a large number of positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions) attracted to each other.  The formula only represents the simplest ratio of the ions and is called the formula unit.  The formula unit, MgCl2, gives the ratio of ions as 1:2, but this does not mean that MgCl2 molecules are present.  The term formula unit is only used when the compound is not made up of molecules.  Names of ionic compounds are derived from the ions of which they are made.  The positive ion name is written first followed by the name of the negative ion. The monatomic positive ion name is simply the name of the metal ion from which it is derived.  

e.g. K+ is potassium,  Na+ is sodium ,  Mg2+ is magnesium and Al3+ is aluminium

When one type of metal atom can have different ionic charges the Roman numeral is added, in brackets, directly after the name.  This occurs with most transition metals.

 e.g. Fe2+  is iron(II),  Fe3+  is iron(III),  Cu2+  is copper(II) and Cu+ is copper(I).


The monatomic negative ion name is half the name of the nonmetal ion with the ending -ide added.  e.g.  O2- is oxide,  S2- is sulphide, N3- is nitride, F- is fluoride and Cl- is chloride.

Polyatomic Ion
A polyatomic ion are atoms bonded covalently that collectively carry a specific charge.  

Brackets must be placed around the polyatomic ion if it occurs more than once in the formula.  These ions behave as individual anions and cations and do not generally break up during the dissolution (dissolving) of a compound.  Treat them as stable units that stay together.
