Polarity and Covalent Bonds

Have you seen a thin stream of water bend around a plastic ruler which has been charged by rubbing? Why does this happen? Does the water comprise of charged particles? We can test this possibility by using a conductivity meter or by attempting to electrolyse the water. The results indicate that there are too few ions to explain the pronounced deflection obtained. Perhaps all streams of liquid will behave in the same way as water! This idea can be tested by placing various liquids separately in burettes and allowing a thin stream to run into a beaker. A charged rod brought close to the stream will produce the evidence needed to answer the question. Why do some liquid streams bend towards a charged rod but not others? Has it something to do with the charge distribution in the molecules of the substance? We need to look for evidence. In order to understand this better some revision of covalent bonding is necessary.  A bond is formed between two atoms because the total energy of the bonded atoms together is lower than that of the unbonded atoms.  Covalent bonding is a result of a pair of electrons being 'shared' between two atoms.  There is no electronegativity difference between atoms of the same element and both atoms attract the shared electrons equally.  The bond is non-polar and in the case of a diatomic molecule, the molecule is therfore non-polar.  However if the atoms are from different elements with different electronegativities the electrons are not shared equally.

Consider an HF molecule.     d+ H -  F  d-

Fluorine is more electronegative than hydrogen in that it attracts the shared pair of electrons better than hydrogen.  The fluorine side of the molecule is the negative side of the molecule while the hydrogen side of the molecule is the positive side of the molecule.  The bond is polar and in this case the molecule is therefore polar.

Geometry of Molecules with three or more atoms

In order to deal with polarity in molecules with 3 or more atoms it is necessary to know the arrangement of atoms relative to one another.

Molecules and polyatomic ions have shapes (geometries). The shapes can be deduced from X-ray studies. For example, the molecular geometry of CO2 is linear, whereas H2O is angular; BF3 is trigonal planar; NH3 is trigonal pyramidal and CH4 is pyramidal.  These geometrical shapes are illustrated below:

The reason for the geometry can be understood by considering orbital theory or more simply by arranging the bonding electron pairs and lone electron pairs in the outer level of electrons so that they are positioned as far away from each other as possible. The electron pairs will adopt a spacial arrangement that minimises electron-pair repulsion around the atom.  This means that the pairs ‘try to avoid’ one another and therefore position themselves so as to make the angles between themselves as large as possible.

Lone pairs repel negative charge more effectively than bonding pairs or can be perceived to require more space about the central atom. If four bonding pairs are arranged around a central atom as in a methane molecule the bond angle is a 109o due to the tetrahedral shape.  As one proceeds from methane to ammonia and then to water the number of lone pairs increases and the bond angle decreases.  Ammonia has a bond angle of 107o and water has a bond angle of 105o.

Polarity in molecules involving three or more atoms

Molecules in which the charge distribution is permanently uneven are said to possess a dipole moment or are said to be polar molecules. This occurs when any polar bonds in the molecule are asymmetrically arranged.  One end of the molecule has a permanent but very small negative charge and the other end has a permanent but very small positive charge. Thin streams of liquids whose molecules are dipoles would be expected to bend around charged rods.
