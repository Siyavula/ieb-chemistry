Objectives

You must be able to:

        Intramolecular Bonds

  • Define an intramolecular bond as occurring between atoms within molecules
  • Define electronegativity as a measure of the tendency of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons
  • Define a covalent bond as a sharing of at least one pair of electrons by two atoms
    • Non - polar covalent (pure covalent) is an equal sharing of electrons
    • Polar covalent is unequal sharing of electrons leading to a dipole forming (as a result of electronegativity difference
  • Define an ionic bond as a transfer of electrons and subsequent electrostatic attraction
  • Define metallic bonding as being between a positive kernel and a sea of delocalized electrons
  • In giant structures such as diamond, graphite and silicon dioxide melting and boiling points are high due to strong covalent bonds being broken
  • Identify that in giant ionic solids (eg sodium chloride), the melting points and boiling points are determined by electrostatic forces of attraction (ionic bonds) between the cations and the anions in the lattice structure

        Intermolecular Forces

  • Identify that in a liquid or a solid there must be forces between the molecules causing them to be attracted to one another, otherwise the molecules would move apart and form a gas
  • Define intermolecular forces as a weak force of attraction between molecules or between atoms of noble gases
  • Distinguish between intermolecular forces and intramolecular bonds
  • Name and explain the origin of the different intermolecular forces:
    • Van der Waal's forces
      1. dipole-dipole
      2. ion-dipole
      3. induced dipole (London forces)
    • Hydrogen bonding as a special case of dipole-dipole forced
  • Identify that within simple molecular substances the melting point and boiling point are affected by the strength (influenced by number of electrons and polarity of molecule) and the relative number of the intermolecular forces

Polarity and Covalent Bonds

Have you seen a thin stream of water bend around a plastic ruler which has been charged by rubbing? Why does this happen? Does the water comprise of charged particles? We can test this possibility by using a conductivity meter or by attempting to electrolyse the water. The results indicate that there are too few ions to explain the pronounced deflection obtained. Perhaps all streams of liquid will behave in the same way as water! This idea can be tested by placing various liquids separately in burettes and allowing a thin stream to run into a beaker. A charged rod brought close to the stream will produce the evidence needed to answer the question. Why do some liquid streams bend towards a charged rod but not others? Has it something to do with the charge distribution in the molecules of the substance? We need to look for evidence. The analysis of an X-ray pattern it is possible to deduce the pattern of electrons in a solid. The electron pattern is usually presented as an electron density map. The results obtained compare well with computer generated pictures based on theoretical considerations.

In order to understand this better some revision of covalent bonding is necessary.  A bond is formed between two atoms because the total energy of the bonded atoms together is lower than that of the unbonded atoms.  Covalent bonding is a result of a pair of electrons being 'shared' between two atoms.


Represent hydrogen chloride ( HCl \text{HCl}) using a Lewis diagram.

For each atom, determine the number of valence electrons in the atom, and represent these using dots and crosses.
